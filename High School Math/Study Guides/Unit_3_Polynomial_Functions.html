Going deeper · Why only the leading term decides end behaviourж·ұе…Ҙ · дёәдҪ•еҸӘжңүйҰ–йЎ№еҶіе®ҡжң«з«ҜиЎҢдёә

Factor $a_{n} x^{n}$ out: $p(x) = a_{n} x^{n} \left( 1 + \frac{a_{n-1}}{a_{n} x} + \cdots + \frac{a_{0}}{a_{n} x^{n}} \right)$. As $|x| \to \infty$, every term in the bracket except the constant $1$ heads to $0$, so the bracket approaches $1$ and $p(x)$ behaves like $a_{n} x^{n}$ for large $|x|$. That is the whole "only the leading term matters" rule: lower-order terms are eventually negligible. If $p(x) = a_{n} x^{n} + \cdots + a_{0}$ has integer coefficients and rational root $p/q$ in lowest terms, then $p(p/q) = 0$. Memorise four facts. (1) The factor theorem: $p(a) = 0$ if and only if $(x - a)$ is a factor of $p(x)$. (2) The remainder theorem: the remainder when $p(x)$ is divided by $(x - a)$ is $p(a)$. (3) End behaviour is set by the leading term: $a_{n} x^{n}$ alone decides what happens as $x \to \pm \infty$. (4) A polynomial of degree $n$ has at most $n - 1$ turning points and exactly $n$ roots counted with multiplicity (in $\mathbb{C}$).
